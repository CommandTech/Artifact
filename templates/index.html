    How to Spot Red Flags

    1. Anonymous or unverifiable author: No byline, vague credentials, or unrelated expertise.
    2. Missing primary sources: Claims without links to original reports, data, or studies.
    3. Cherry-picked data: Selective graphs, no baseline, or axes that start at non-zero to exaggerate effects.
    4. Overblown headlines: Sensational language that the body text doesn't support.
    5. Out-of-context quotes: Snippets that change meaning when read in full.
    6. Stock photos as evidence: Images that don't document the actual event or timeline.
    7. Broken/opaque links: Links that redirect oddly, are paywalled without context, or point to unrelated pages.
    8. Conflict of interest undisclosed: Affiliate links, sponsored content, or funding ties not stated.
    9. No dates / stale info: Time-free claims presented as current.
    10. One-source stories: No corroboration from independent outlets.

    Tip: Use the article's claim as a search query + “site:.gov”, “site:.edu”, or “filetype:pdf” to look for higher-quality corroboration.
